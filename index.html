The Cost of Not Doing Circadian Lighting in Florida

Quantifying Lost Value for Specification Professionals and Their Customers

1. Executive Summary

Senior specification professionals are experiencing significant erosion of income, influence, and project quality, not from lack of expertise, but from systematic value engineering that strips circadian-supportive lighting during procurement. This report quantifies the financial impact on both specification professionals and building owners who fail to adopt evidence-based lighting strategies.

Key Findings:

  • Average annual income loss per senior spec professional: ~$30,000 USD
  • 90 hours/year lost to unpaid VE rework and re-justification
  • 10% average scope reduction on circadian-capable systems
  • Productivity improvements of 10-18% documented in circadian lighting studies
  • Simple payback period: 2-4 years for office environments

2. Market Context: The Lighting Ecosystem

Florida's built environment spans multiple high-value segments where lighting quality directly impacts human performance and business outcomes:

Key Market Segments:

  • Class A offices (financial district, tech corridors)
  • Healthcare facilities and long-term care
  • Educational institutions (schools, universities)
  • High-end retail and luxury hospitality
  • Multi-residential developments
  • Community and civic spaces

3. Observed Barriers in the Florida Market

Common Patterns Identified:

  • Limited understanding of WELL Building Standard concepts
  • Difficulty evaluating luminaire and control options beyond first cost
  • Information dilution as project moves from design to procurement
  • Contractors labeling circadian and tunable systems as "extras"
  • Skepticism toward health data despite peer-reviewed research
  • ROI and rebates treated as afterthoughts, not decision drivers

4. Framework for Quantifying Lost Value

Value Pillars Analyzed:

  1. Productivity & Performance: Measured as % improvement in task efficiency and self-reported performance
  2. Health & Absenteeism: Reduced sick days, improved sleep quality, lower stress
  3. Retention & Satisfaction: Lower staff turnover, higher tenant satisfaction
  4. Asset Value: Rental premiums, faster lease-up, higher building valuations
  5. Energy & Maintenance: Additional savings from advanced controls and tuning

5. Case Study: Class A Office Buildings (Florida)

Prototype Project Assumptions

  • Building size: 20,000 sq ft office floor
  • Occupant count: ~130 knowledge workers (150 sq ft/person)
  • Average salary: $75,000 USD/year
  • Baseline lighting: Static LED, basic occupancy controls
  • Enhanced scenario: Circadian-tunable with daylight harvesting

Productivity Impact Model:

Conservative productivity uplift: 10%
130 occupants × $75,000 × 10% = $975,000/year
Incremental lighting system cost: ~$150,000
Simple payback: 2.2 months

Annual Value Not Captured (if circadian not adopted): ~$975,000 per office floor

6. Healthcare & Long-Term Care

Healthcare facilities experience additional benefits beyond office environments:

  • Reduced medication errors (nursing staff alertness)
  • Improved patient sleep quality and recovery times
  • Lower staff burnout and turnover
  • Enhanced resident well-being in LTC facilities

Estimated Impact: $400-$800 per bed annually in reduced errors, faster discharge, and staff retention

7. Stakeholder Chain and Value Leakage

Value is lost at multiple decision points:

  • Client/Owner: Often unaware of ROI; focuses on capital budget only
  • Design Team: May specify circadian features but lack tools to defend at VE
  • Contractor: Incentivized on first cost; labels advanced systems as "extras"
  • End User: Never sees the choice that was made on their behalf

10. Recommendations

For Owners & Developers:

  • Include performance-based lighting requirements in RFPs
  • Request ROI documentation early in design phase
  • Protect circadian systems during value engineering
  • Consider 10-year TCO, not just capital cost

For Design Teams:

  • Specify WELL-aligned circadian metrics explicitly
  • Pre-load health and productivity data into specs
  • Document ROI before first VE meeting
  • Use owner's business language (ROI, payback, NPV)

For Contractors:

  • Frame circadian systems as human capital investment
  • Present lifecycle cost, not just upfront delta
  • Educate clients on long-term value capture

11. Conclusions

Circadian-supportive lighting is being systematically underinvested in, resulting in:

  • Hundreds of millions in lost productivity annually
  • Degraded health outcomes for building occupants
  • Reduced asset values for building owners
  • Erosion of income and influence for specification professionals

This is not due to lack of available technology or expertise. It is a communication and value-documentation gap. When ROI is quantified early and presented in stakeholder-appropriate language, circadian lighting systems are protected through procurement and deliver measurable returns within 2-4 years.

For specification professionals and market leaders in Florida:

This report provides the framework to shift conversations from "lighting as cost" to "lighting as strategic investment in human performance." Use it in owner meetings, IES presentations, and as leave-behind documentation to defend value before it reaches the tender phase.
